Hm, not quite sure about current procedures followed by Baan but:
a) Standard functionality: This is the way the current software works (by model restrictions, by design decisions, by theoretic constraints (like MRP etc), by technical posibilities, too many customers would run into problems if it was going to be changed in the way you want it). If you (still) want it the other way, you should not turn to Baan but turn to a customizing partner or do your own customization. There is no way to have it working the way you want it in the standard software.

b) A bit like a), but they might consider to have it changed in newer versions, good suggestion. A defect (prio 50) is made and transfered to the development department. The problem is not going to be solved in your version, so you do not need to wait for that. Your case is closed.

A feedback may cause them to review their initial solution (and have the case "reopened").

Don't be so naive.

The support organisation is managed by "metrics" which were introduced by a (VERY) former VP who's favourite quote was "If I can't measure it, I can't manage it".

The metrics change from time to time, but they publish the changes internally. To look good you need to know the metrics, not the baan software.

The job of a support analyst is to close the case, not solve the problem. Many ex-baan guys will be able to quote you case numbers where the closing dialogue is something like:

"If I don't hear from you in the next two days I will assume that the problem is solved" (Friday, 6 p.m.)

"As I have had no response to my last comment I have closed your case and put it into status 'soltion accepted'. Thank you for your custom" (Monday, 9.01 a.m.)

Don't get me wrong - there are some VERY VERY good support analysts in the company, even now. If you've connected with one you'll know what I'm talking about. But there are an inordinate number of "metrics chasers" too.
