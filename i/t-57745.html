The difference between an enhancement request and a defect is whether or not the session is working as designed.

For example, let's say that when you create your database user, you think that baan should assign the DB user a random password instead of letting the sysadmin enter one. Local law may require it, security experts may say "that's absolutely required", and it may make absolute sense. However, that's not a DEFECT. The session was designed to allow you to enter the password. It is doing that and doing it well.

It's important to get fixed - and it will - changes like that are accepted, but they go into the next release.

You save the changes in functionality for major releases, you fix the bugs in the code in minor releases for the version you're on.

That's the standard SDLC, that's what the software industry has been using as it's basis for years and years. Look at any common software product, you don't see oracle releasing a new, better way, to handle datafiles in a point release.

Hi Saikat,

The reason that changes in functionality, or design issues are considered an enhancement is that it will take serious time, thought, effort and QA to resolve the problem. unlike a bug, they can't locate a flaw in a chunk of code, fix that, unit test, regression test and then release a patch.

To redesign a process they have to do a macro/micro design, this involves going to SMEs for the particular area (for example indian localization they may need market research, or legal input) followed by extensive coding, unit test, regression test, integration test, etc.

If you're familiar with the SDLC then you should realize how developers work. You can only create a defect against a piece of code if the code isn't compliant with the requirements. If the requirements don't cover something, than that is a recognized problem - but it's not a bug. It's usually a Change Request or something similar. It requires the Business Analysts to get involved to help determine the solution.

My experience with Baan support is that if you can clearly articulate the problem, are able to reproduce it, give them the appropriate traces and an actual error is being generated that most support folk will log it as a defect and it will get resolved for your current release (eventually).

(Please note, I do not work for, or represent Infor)

There are a couple of things I'd like to state here.

I perceive the Infor support from working with it in various roles, namely as a customer myself, as a consultant working for my customers, and also as a subcontractor working for Infor partners.

The thing with support is, it really depends. It depends on who you get as your first level contact. There are very experienced guys and girls there, but also some, that are really a pain to work with. Unfortunately, those are the ones that stay in memory.

It also depends on how well you qualify your incident and if you are able to reproduce it.

And - and that's understandable but questionable - it really depends on how strategic you are as a customer for Infor. The rule is: First the big Infor customers, than the smaller Infor customers, then the big customers of the partners, then the small Infor customers. If you're a small customer of a partner, your chances getting a bugfix for a malfunctioning software functionality that only you are using are close to zero. Paying support fees or not - Infor is BIG ERP here.

In the percentage of incidents I filed where I knew that I was talking about a bug (and I am a qualified consultant with more than ten years of experience, I don't file an incident withount *knowing* that something's fishy), I'd say (from feeling, I have no metrics) that only in 10% of those cases I got my solution quick and without cumbersome discussions.


I had situations, where three different customers reported a situation as a bug, that could be fixed with probably a single line of code (DEM ignoring tools authorizations): "Works as specified, will not be fixed".

I had situations, where I could pinpoint a bug indicating the line number of the source where the bug was, and it still took a lot of time to get a solution - a solution that was implemented differently and did not really solve the issue.

I had situations where I could pinpoint a solution number that made functionality, which was available before, but seldom used, not working any longer. And got long statement back from the supporters, that this wasn't a bug, but intended functionality. They did not even look on my paper.

I had situations where I needed a couple of hours to explain the software functionality to the supporter.

I had situations where a bug was admitted to be a bug by Infor, but was not fixed because this would be too complicated and my customer was to small. It was an issue where sales turnover statistics are wrong when using schedules and cross-period deliveries, so, Infor, if you really want to get into the automotive sector, you should better care for that issue...

Oh, by the way: That was one of the few automotive projects, and an early one, and I had to iron out about five severe bugs in the sales and purchase schedule functionality. It was really a pain to get something through the firewalls and to the guys who could fix it. In such a situation, where Infor is *knowing* they delivered banana software (ripening at the customer's site) that no customer is using yet, I'd really expect the firewalls between customers/consultants and development pretty low, but in particular in this project it was always hours of talking on the phone to get the supporter to file a defect.

On the other side, my dear customers:

I've seen customers filing support incidents like "Order 4711 cannot be processed"

I've seen customers filing support incidents for every missing label to collect ammunition against Infor when fighting about project costs

I've seen customers on veeery old service packs filing incidents.

I've heard from Infor that about 80% of the incidents are NOT bugs, but handling problems, enhancement requests etc.


In such a situation, dear customers, what do you expect a support consultant is used to: That a filed incident is a bug, or that a filed incident is "just another of those silly RTFMs".


But again, on the other side, dear Infor: Against what criteria are you measuring your support personel: How fast they close the incidents. Thats ok. But, as far as I've heard, also how often they file a defect. And that's a veeery bad metric. Because it makes filing many defects bad for the supportes. So they don't do it. And that's exactly why the Infor customers perceive the Infor support as lousy: The supporters are not seen as a helping hand, but as bouncers standing between a problem and it's solution.
