Riaz

I suspect that your MAUC problem is that returns to stock via service and sales are not costed transactions.

Whereas your purchase order receipt has a known cost associated with it that can be used to (re)calculate the MAUC, returns do not. So, having issued all your stock at a MAUC of 20, the MAUC reverts to 0 because a MAUC can only exist where there is a positive stock balance. I think that returns and stock adjustments inflate stock without changing the MAUC, because otherwise (when stock and MAUC are positive) such transactions would dilute the MAUC, and artificially depressing costs of transactions.

(Hi Riaz. I know you sent me private message on this issue, but let me just reply here for the benefit of everybody)

Indeed, for all returns in BaanERP (at least in the heavily patched 5.0c system that we implemented) the return stock will be valued at standard price as Service girl observed before. Now it's not easy valuing return stock from accounting point of view. It's probably fine if, like in Riaz's particular case, the return is made right after the issue. But what if the return is made long after the issue, and in meantime there were already so many other issues? What value should you take in? That being told, probably the most appropriate way to value return stock if average costing is used is the latest MAUC as Jftello said.

Just a little note; MAUC is only updated upon receipt of goods, such as purchase receipt, service return, etc. So what happened in your case, Riaz, was:
The purchase receipt updated the MAUC to USD 20/pcs
Issue was made at the MAUC then, at USD 20/pcs. No update was made on MAUC this time
Service return was valued at standard price USD 0/pcs. Hence, MAUC updated to USD 0/pcs

MAUC stands for the Moving Average Unit Cost, and is updated for each receipt.

The calculation method is as follows:
MAUC = (old on hand * old MAUC) + (receipt quantity * receipt price) / (old on
hand + inbound quantity)
